Despite sustained economic growth in Peru, inequality remains a persistent and multidimensional phenomenon that shapes both material conditions and individual interpretations of opportunity and the role of the state. Using nationally representative data from the ENADES surveys (2022–2024), this study proposes a two-stage analytical framework that combines deep representation learning with unsupervised clustering to analyze inequality perceptions. In the first stage, a flexible autoencoder constructs a low-dimensional latent space that captures complex nonlinear relationships across attitudinal variables. In the second stage, K-means clustering identifies groups of individuals with internally consistent belief configurations, with model selection guided by multiple internal validation metrics. The results reveal a structural shift in the organization of perceptions over time. In 2022, attitudes are fragmented into six overlapping profiles, reflecting heterogeneous and weakly aligned belief systems. By 2024, the structure becomes more consolidated, with four more coherent and clearly differentiated profiles. Feature importance analysis shows a transition from an economic–evaluative logic—centered on inequality levels, mobility, and redistribution—toward a more interpretive framework, where beliefs about individual responsibility and opportunity play a central role. Although the cross-sectional nature of the data limits causal interpretation, the study provides a transparent and replicable framework for uncovering latent attitudinal structures. By integrating deep learning and clustering methods, it contributes to a better understanding of how inequality perceptions evolve and how they relate to underlying social and territorial conditions.The implementation and code supporting this analysis are publicly available at https://github.com/AlejandroAybar-Flores/Latent-Attitudinal-ProfilesPeru.
